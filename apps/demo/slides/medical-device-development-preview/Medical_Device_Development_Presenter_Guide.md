# Medical Device Development - 90-Minute Presenter Guide

Audience: international youth visitors at NCKU, mixed frontline healthcare, Ministry / HMIS, nurses, paramedics, radiology, OT, lab, pharmacy, engineering / BME students.

Purpose: practical, respectful design-thinking lecture for resource-constrained health systems. The message is not "copy high-income-country MedTech." The message is: understand how industrial technologies become regulated medical systems, then design a device that can survive the real setting, facility, and health system.

## 90-Minute Run Sheet

| Time | Slides | Mode | Presenter Intention |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 0-5 | 1-2 | Welcome + route map | Establish trust, topic, and the three-part journey. |
| 5-13 | 3-5 | Technology evolution opener | Show medical devices as industrial technologies translated into care, using international company examples. |
| 13-22 | 6-8 | Global ecosystem opener | Explain industry, regulators, standards, notified bodies, testing, and evidence chain before going into frameworks. |
| 22-28 | 9-10 | History + thesis | Explain why regulation evolved, then state: a medical device is a system. |
| 28-38 | 11-14 | Audience fit + foundations + Interaction 1 | Build the core idea: intended use changes everything. |
| 38-45 | 15-17 | Risk + regulation primer | Lower regulatory anxiety by turning it into four questions. |
| 45-56 | 18-23 | Five-family map + translation | Show the framework landscape, but tell learners not to memorize names. |
| 56-68 | 24-30 | Evidence, safety, SaMD, value | Give enough theory to be credible without drowning non-engineers. |
| 68-76 | 31-36 | Adoption + LMIC cases + decision guide | Move from invention to survival in real systems. |
| 76-81 | 37-39 | VRMT stakeholder map | Use VRMT as a compact example of stakeholder mapping, not as the only priority. |
| 81-89 | 40-42 | Interaction 2 | Mixed challenge teams: design for survival, then name one harm and mitigation. |
| 89-90 | 43-44 | Close | One reflective question and the five-family checklist to take home. |

## Opening Frame

Suggested opening:

"Medical devices are not born only inside hospitals. They often begin as industrial capabilities: materials, precision mechanics, electronics, semiconductors, robotics, cloud, and AI. The hard part is translating those capabilities into safe, useful, maintainable care."

Warmth cue:

"Many of you already know more about your setting than any outside designer. I will provide a framework; you will provide reality."

## Slide-By-Slide Delivery Notes

| Slide | Timing | How to Deliver |
|---:|---:|---|
| 1. Medical Device Development | 2 min | Introduce yourself, NCKU / MDIC / WTMH, and why your work sits between engineering, clinics, and implementation. |
| 2. What we will do together | 3 min | Name the three movements: device or not, design safely, survival test. Point out the two interactions early. |
| 3. Medical devices are industrial technologies translated into care | 3 min | Give the big thesis: industrial capability becomes medical responsibility. This is the international frame. |
| 4. Seven technology waves created seven device families | 3 min | Scan the grid. Do not read all companies; choose one familiar example per row if needed. |
| 5. Each wave put a different kind of company on stage | 2 min | Use this as the keynote story: materials, instrumentation, miniaturization, robotics, software-first MedTech. |
| 6. The medical-device world is an ecosystem, not one market | 3 min | Move from technology history to the global stage: platforms, integrators, buyers, service, and the hidden system. |
| 7. Who decides whether a device is safe enough? | 3 min | Explain the center: the manufacturer owns the claim and evidence. Regulators, standards, notified bodies, and users ask different questions around that center. |
| 8. Third parties turn device claims into usable evidence | 3 min | Walk left to right through the evidence chain. Emphasize that third parties support trust, but responsibility stays with the manufacturer. |
| 9. Medical-device regulation grew from harm, complexity, and software | 2 min | Use the timeline to explain why these frameworks exist. History makes regulation feel purposeful, not bureaucratic. |
| 10. A medical device is a system, not a product | 3 min | This is the thesis slide. Slow down. Same device, different system, different outcome. |
| 11. This room already contains a device-development team | 2 min | Make participants feel seen by role, not nationality. Mention clinical, system, and engineering perspectives. |
| 12. Design constraints are not weaknesses | 2 min | Use the dashboard as a visual scan: power, repair, procurement, people, environment. Say these are design requirements. |
| 13. What is a medical device? | 3 min | Explain intended use in plain English. Same object, different claim, different regulatory meaning. |
| 14. Live vote: device or not? | 5 min | Interaction 1. Ask for hand votes first, then 30-second neighbor explanation. Do not correct too quickly. |
| 15. More possible harm means more evidence | 2 min | Use the harm-evidence ladder: thermometer, pulse oximeter, infusion pump / insulin pump. Avoid regulator detail. |
| 16. Regulation starts with four decisions | 3 min | Translate regulation into four questions: claim, risk, route, evidence. This calms non-regulatory learners. |
| 17. Most countries ask for the same logic | 2 min | Emphasize "adapt, do not copy." Reliance and technical-file logic can help smaller systems. |
| 18. Five families of frameworks | 2 min | Master map only. Say: "You do not need to memorize names. Use them as lenses." |
| 19. The design thinking family | 2 min | Rapid scan. Family A is for finding the right problem through observation. |
| 20. Design thinking alone will not give you a device | 1 min | Important boundary. Design thinking starts the journey but does not replace evidence, risk, and adoption work. |
| 21. The translation family | 2 min | Keep it practical: clinic before whiteboard, prototype before spending too much. |
| 22. Translate - Identify, Invent, Implement | 2 min | Photo-this-process slide. Three verbs learners can remember. |
| 23. Which translation pathway, when? | 2 min | Use as a decision menu. Do not over-explain Stanford / MIT / Cambridge origins. |
| 24. Regulatory engineering is the rails | 2 min | Plain-English takeaway for each: QMS controls work, risk file protects patients, usability protects real users. |
| 25. The waterfall is a traceability machine | 3 min | Use the traceability matrix: need -> requirement -> test evidence. |
| 26. Safety evidence must tell one story | 3 min | Use the diagram to connect risk loop, software lifecycle, usability, maintenance, and PMS. |
| 27. SaMD risk depends on the clinical decision | 2 min | Use radiology, HMIS, EMS, and maternity examples. Ask what decision the software influences. |
| 28. The business validation family | 1 min | Rapid slide. One message: who pays, who owns, who maintains. |
| 29. Lean Startup is dangerous in MedTech - until you adapt it | 1 min | Say patients are not disposable beta users. Test assumptions safely and ethically. |
| 30. Fit the organisation or fail | 2 min | Use as bridge from product design to implementation. "Fit organization or fail" is the anchor. |
| 31. NASSS heatmap - quick survival scan | 1 min | Do not read every cell. Teach red-cell scanning only. |
| 32. How to read the heatmap in 60 seconds | 1 min | Green / yellow / red. A red cell becomes a design requirement, not a blame label. |
| 33. The device graveyard is usually a system mismatch | 1 min | Respectful wording: not user failure, but mismatch between device assumptions and system reality. |
| 34. Four cases that fit resource-constrained health systems | 2 min | Case gallery. Pick the example closest to the audience and explain the system evidence. |
| 35. Adoption is not marketing - it is survival | 1 min | NASSS predicts trouble, diffusion chooses early champions, RE-AIM keeps score. |
| 36. Take-home decision guide - which family leads now? | 1 min | Tell learners to photograph this flow: use it for invention, donation, procurement, and student projects. |
| 37. VR Mirror Therapy through five lenses | 2 min | Clarify VRMT is a stakeholder-mapping example, not a claim that VR is every setting's top priority. |
| 38. One device, five stakeholders at work | 2 min | Pick two roles to explain deeply: OT and nurse, or manager and regulator. Do not read all five. |
| 39. VRMT evidence board - what each role asks for | 1 min | The transferable lesson: different stakeholders ask different evidence questions. |
| 40. Mixed challenge teams, not country teams | 2 min | Set norms: mixed roles, no country winners or losers, compete on usefulness and survivability. |
| 41. Small-group challenge - design for survival | 6 min | Run the timer tightly. If time is short, use one problem area and two teams only. |
| 42. Where could your prototype hurt someone? | 2 min | Every team names one harm and one mitigation. High-severity harm must be controlled before patient contact. |
| 43. Three questions for your setting | 0.5 min | Use only if time remains: ask one person to answer "owner + backup owner." |
| 44. Carry this five-family checklist home | 0.5 min | Close with the checklist. Ask learners to use it on one real problem from their own setting. |

## Interaction Facilitation

### Interaction 1: Device or Not? (Slide 14, 5-6 minutes)

Flow:

1. "Vote first. No explanation yet."
2. "Turn to one neighbor. What claim changed your answer?"
3. Take one answer from a clinical learner and one from a system / engineering learner.
4. Close: "The object did not change. The intended use changed. That is why medical-device development starts with claims, users, setting, and risk."

Avoid:

- Do not make it a regulatory trivia quiz.
- Do not embarrass wrong answers. The ambiguity is the teaching point.

### Interaction 2: Design for Survival (Slides 40-42, 8-12 minutes)

Flow:

1. Slide 40: assign mixed challenge teams by problem area, not country.
2. Slide 41: run a compressed 0-1 / 1-4 / 4-7 minute timer if needed.
3. Slide 42: each team names one harm and one mitigation.
4. If time allows, two teams pitch for one minute each.

Recognition language:

"This is not a competition for the most advanced technology. It is a competition for the most survivable, useful, and respectful design."

## If Running Short

Cut or compress in this order:

1. Slide 4: scan only three waves instead of seven.
2. Slides 19, 21, 23: say "framework examples" and move on.
3. Slides 28-29: combine into one sentence on payer, owner, and ethical testing.
4. Slides 31-32: show heatmap, explain red-cell rule only.
5. Slides 38-39: explain only two VRMT stakeholders.

Do not cut:

- Slides 3-5: technology evolution opener.
- Slides 6-8: global ecosystem opener.
- Slide 10: main thesis.
- Slide 14: intended-use interaction.
- Slide 36: take-home decision guide.
- Slides 41-42: group challenge + harm mitigation.
- Slide 44: checklist close.

## Closing Line

"A medical device survives only when all five families are handled: real need, feasible translation, safety evidence, value and ownership, and adoption in the system that will actually run it."
